# VIDEO SCRIPT: "You're Using AI Wrong"

## Runtime Target: 8-10 minutes
## Format: Talking Head + Screen Demo

---

## [HOOK] — 0:00 - 0:35

**[ON CAMERA — direct to camera, confident energy]**

Most people use AI like they're texting a magic genie.

*[slight pause]*

They type something vague... hit enter... and wonder why they got garbage back.

*[lean in slightly]*

Here's the problem: AI isn't a mind reader. It's more like a really smart employee who just started day one — and has absolutely zero context about your life, your business, or what you actually need.

Today I'm going to show you the one mental shift that changes everything.

And it has nothing to do with "better prompts."

---

## [CONTEXT] — 0:35 - 2:15

**[ON CAMERA — conversational, slightly frustrated tone]**

So here's what happens to most people:

They open ChatGPT. They ask it something like "help me with my marketing." They get some generic response. They try again. Same thing. Eventually they think "eh, AI is overhyped" and go back to doing everything manually.

*[VISUAL: Show quick screen recording of vague prompt → generic response]*

I call this "chat mode." And it's a trap.

Here's why it happens: We've been trained by Google to ask *questions*. "What's the best restaurant near me?" "How do I fix this error?" 

But AI isn't Google. You're not searching — you're *directing*.

*[beat]*

And the people who get left behind? They're the busy ones. The managers. The people who don't have time to sit there experimenting for hours. 

They tried AI once, it didn't work, and now they're convinced it's not for them.

That's who I'm making this video for.

So what's the actual fix?

*[slight pause, shift tone to more positive]*

It's not a hack. It's not a magic prompt template. It's a mindset shift.

---

## [CORE CONTENT - PART 1] — 2:15 - 4:00
### The Day-One Employee Framework

**[ON CAMERA]**

Think about this: If you hired a brilliant new employee — smart, capable, fast — but it was their first day...

Would you walk up to them and say "Hey, handle the marketing"?

*[pause for effect]*

No. That would be insane. They'd have no idea what your business does, who your customers are, what's worked before, what your goals are.

You'd give them *context* first.

**[SCREEN DEMO — side by side comparison]**

*[Show ChatGPT or Claude interface]*

Here's what most people type:

> "Write me a marketing email"

And here's what they *should* be typing:

> "I run a small landscaping company in Austin. We just added a new service — outdoor lighting installation. I need an email to send to our existing customers (homeowners who've used our lawn care services). The email should be excited but not salesy, about 3 paragraphs, and end with a clear call-to-action to schedule a consultation. Our brand voice is friendly and professional."

**[ON CAMERA]**

Same request. Completely different results.

The difference isn't magic. It's context.

*[gesture]*

Context is the unlock. Give AI the same information you'd give that new employee on day one.

---

## [CORE CONTENT - PART 2] — 4:00 - 5:45
### Tasks, Not Wishes

**[ON CAMERA]**

Okay, so context is step one. Step two is even simpler:

Stop making wishes. Start giving tasks.

*[demonstrate with hands]*

A wish sounds like: "Help me with my presentation."

A task sounds like: "Create a 10-slide outline for a presentation about our Q1 results. Include sections on: revenue growth, new customers, challenges we faced, and priorities for Q2. Make the tone confident but realistic."

**[SCREEN DEMO]**

*[Show a real example of a vague request vs a specific task]*

*[Show the dramatically different outputs]*

**[ON CAMERA]**

See the difference? 

One is hoping AI reads your mind. The other is directing AI like you would a capable teammate.

*[lean in]*

And here's the thing most people miss: being specific actually saves you time. Because you're not going back and forth twenty times trying to fix something that was broken from the start.

---

## [CORE CONTENT - PART 3] — 5:45 - 7:30
### Direction, Not Dictation

**[ON CAMERA]**

Alright, last piece. And this is the big one.

*[pause for emphasis]*

Most people think AI should give them the perfect answer on the first try. When it doesn't, they give up.

But that's not how it works. Here's the real workflow:

**[VISUAL: Simple graphic showing loop]**
1. Give AI context and a clear task
2. Get a first draft (probably 70-80% there)
3. Tell it what to fix
4. Get a better version
5. Repeat until it's right

**[ON CAMERA]**

This is the mindset shift: You're not the *typer*. You're the *director*.

*[gesture like a director]*

You set the vision. You give the context. AI does the heavy lifting. You review and refine.

**[SCREEN DEMO — show an iteration example]**

*[Show initial output]*
*[Show revision request: "This is good but make the opening more direct and cut the third paragraph — it's too generic"]*
*[Show improved output]*

**[ON CAMERA]**

THIS is how people actually get value from AI. Not from one magic prompt. From an *iterative process* where you stay in control.

And once you internalize this... 

*[pause]*

You stop chatting with AI. And you start building *systems* with it.

That's the real game. And that's what we're going to be exploring on this channel.

---

## [CALLBACK/CTA] — 7:30 - 8:30

**[ON CAMERA — wrapping up, warm energy]**

Alright, let's bring it home.

Three things to remember:

*[count on fingers]*

**One:** AI isn't magic. It's a capable employee on day one. Give it context.

**Two:** Stop wishing. Start tasking. Be specific about what you want.

**Three:** You're the director. Give direction, review the output, iterate until it's right.

*[direct to camera, genuine]*

If this clicked for you — if you've been frustrated with AI and this gave you a new way to think about it — subscribe. 

I'm going to be sharing more about how to take AI from "cool chat tool" to actual systems that run in the background while you do real work.

*[optional comment prompt]*

And drop a comment: What's ONE task you've been trying to get AI to do that never works right? Let's figure it out together.

*[beat, then sign off]*

I'll see you in the next one.
