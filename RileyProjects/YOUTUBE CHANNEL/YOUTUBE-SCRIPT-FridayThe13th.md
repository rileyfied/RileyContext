*[gesture with three fingers]*

**Anthropic** said: we're all about scale. Thirty billion dollars in funding. Enterprise everything.

**Google** said: forget speed — we're building AI that actually *thinks*.

## [CORE CONTENT - PART 1: OpenAI = Speed] — 2:00 - 4:00

**[ON CAMERA]**

First up: OpenAI.

They released something called GPT 5.3 Codex. Now, I know a lot of you aren't developers, but stay with me because this matters even if you never touch code.

Here's the idea: instead of AI that writes one thing and waits for you to ask for another thing, they built AI that can *manage an entire workflow*.

Think about it like this.

*[analogy mode]*

Old AI was like asking someone to proofread an email. You send it to them, they send back corrections, you make changes, you send it again.

New AI is like handing someone the entire email chain and saying "handle this conversation for me — just check in when you need a decision."

**[SCREEN SHARE — show Codex interface if available, or mockup]**

They call it "agentic coding." The AI can plan, execute, see errors, and fix itself without you babysitting every step.

For non-tech people, here's why this matters: this isn't just about code. This pattern — AI managing multi-step workflows autonomously — is coming to *everything*. Your email. Your scheduling. Your project management.

OpenAI is betting the future is speed. Fast AI that can handle complex tasks without constant hand-holding.

**But** — and this is the catch — there's a cost.

*[ON CAMERA, more serious]*

They're also retiring old AI models aggressively. Meaning if you built something on their platform six months ago, it might not work next month.

Speed comes with chaos.

If you're using OpenAI tools, you need to stay on your toes. Don't assume what works today will work tomorrow.

---

## [CORE CONTENT - PART 2: Anthropic = Scale + Policy] — 4:00 - 6:00

**[ON CAMERA]**

Second: Anthropic.

You might know them as the company behind Claude — the "safe" AI, the "thoughtful" AI.

On Friday, they announced a $30 billion funding round.

Let me say that again. Thirty. Billion. Dollars.

*[pause for effect]*

That's not just money to buy servers. That's a bet on enterprise scale. They're positioning Claude deep into corporate tools, coding assistants, partnerships with every platform you can think of.

Their vision: AI everywhere, integrated into everything you already use.

Sounds good, right?

Here's the tension.

**[SCREEN SHARE — show headlines about defense policy controversy]**

Anthropic has very strict policies about what you can and can't do with their AI. They have a "constitution" for Claude. Rules about safety, ethics, acceptable use.

And right now, there's a huge debate about whether AI companies should work with defense and military.

*[ON CAMERA]*

Now, you might be thinking: "I'm not building weapons. Why do I care?"

Here's why you care.

Policy is now operational risk.

If you're using Claude for customer support, and tomorrow Anthropic tightens their safety rules to avoid controversy, your totally harmless use case might suddenly start getting blocked.

*[example]*

"I'm sorry, I can't help with that request because my safety settings flag it as potentially sensitive."

That's not a hypothetical. That's a real risk when you're buying into a vendor's philosophy, not just their technology.

So Anthropic's strategy is scale — but with strings attached. You're not just adopting their AI. You're adopting their values. And if those values shift, your product might break.

---

## [CORE CONTENT - PART 3: Google = Deep Reasoning] — 6:00 - 7:30

**[ON CAMERA]**

Third: Google DeepMind.

While OpenAI and Anthropic are racing to be the fastest, most integrated AI assistant, Google made a completely different move.

They announced something called Deep Think — part of their Gemini 3 initiative.

And the pitch is basically: "We're done with the chatbot wars. We're building AI that actually thinks."

*[ON CAMERA — explain the difference]*

Most AI right now is optimized for speed. You ask a question, you get an answer in two seconds.

Google's bet: for the really important stuff, you don't want speed. You want accuracy. You want deep reasoning.

**[SCREEN SHARE — comparison graphic]**

Fast AI: "Write me an email."  
Reasoning AI: "Solve this unsolved math theorem."

Fast AI: "Summarize this article."  
Reasoning AI: "Design a new pharmaceutical compound."

Google is targeting science, research, engineering — problems where you'd rather wait 30 seconds and get the *right* answer than get a fast wrong answer.

*[ON CAMERA]*

For most of us, this doesn't change much right now. But it's a signal.

The AI market is splitting.

On one side: fast, general-purpose AI for everyday tasks.  
On the other: slow, deliberate AI for high-stakes decisions.

And depending on what you're building, you're going to need to pick your lane.

---

## [CALLBACK/SO WHAT] — 7:30 - 9:00

**[ON CAMERA — wrap it up, bring it home]**

Okay. Let's zoom out.

Why does this Friday the 13th announcement spree actually matter?

Here's the bottom line: for two years, everyone in AI was building the same thing. Better chatbots. Faster responses. Smoother interfaces.

That era is over.

Now we have three paths:

**Path 1 — OpenAI:** Speed. Move fast, iterate constantly, accept chaos.  
**Path 2 — Anthropic:** Scale with safety. Enterprise integration, but with policy constraints.  
**Path 3 — Google:** Deep reasoning. Slow, deliberate, high-accuracy AI for complex problems.

*[pause]*

So what should you do?

If you're already using AI tools, ask yourself: which path are you on? And is that the right path for what you're actually trying to build?

If you need speed and you're willing to adapt constantly — OpenAI is your bet.  
If you need enterprise reliability and you're okay with ethical guardrails — Anthropic is your bet.  
If you're solving hard, complex problems where accuracy matters more than speed — Google is your bet.

But here's the thing nobody's saying out loud: **you might need more than one**.

The idea that one AI does everything? That's dying.

The future is picking the right tool for the right job. And as of Friday the 13th, those tools are diverging fast.
